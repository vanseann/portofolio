Stellar Distance Estimation from Gaia DR3

Background

Accurate determination of stellar distances is essential for understanding the structure of our Galaxy and stellar populations. Gaia DR3 provides precise parallaxes for millions of stars, but uncertainties in parallax measurements necessitate probabilistic methods for distance estimation.

Methods

Distances were estimated using a Bayesian framework. For each star:

  • The likelihood was computed assuming a Gaussian distribution of the observed parallax and its uncertainty.
  • Three different priors on the distance were considered: uniform, constant volume density, and exponentially decreasing volume density.
  • The posterior distribution was calculated as the product of the likelihood and the prior, and normalized.
  • The mode of the posterior was taken as the best distance estimate, and uncertainties were quantified using the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the posterior.
  • Visualization of the likelihood, prior, posterior, and uncertainty was performed for each star.

Results

  • The exponentially decreasing volume density prior generally provides the most physically realistic distance estimates for Gaia parallaxes, especially when relative parallax errors are moderate to large.
  • The constant-volume-density prior can be useful in some regimes but has limitations: when parallax uncertainties are large it can produce unreliable distance constraints compared to the exponential prior.
  • Stars with very large relative parallax errors may yield broad or weakly constrained posteriors; in such cases the choice of prior strongly influences the inferred distance.
  • Negative parallaxes cannot be interpreted directly as physical distances; they lead to non-physical estimates under naive inversion and must be treated with a full probabilistic approach (likelihood × prior). Our pipeline flags or excludes cases where the posterior mode is non-physical.
  • Distance estimates vary depending on the chosen prior, illustrating the impact of prior assumptions on inference.
  • Uncertainties were systematically computed and displayed for all stars, facilitating comparison across different priors.
  • We provide automated, publication-quality plots for likelihoods, priors, posteriors, and derived uncertainties (FWHM) so users can inspect cases where priors dominate the inference.
